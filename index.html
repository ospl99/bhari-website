My Story

From building companies to backing them.

In 1986, I co-founded Ontrack Systems in Kolkata and spent 27 years scaling it across enterprise software. Along the way, I built Calcutta Online in 1996 — one of India's earliest internet ventures. Time Magazine featured it, and it was eventually acquired by a NASDAQ-listed company.

That journey — the fundraising, the pivots, the loneliness of early-stage building — shaped how I invest today. I don't sit across the table from founders. I sit beside them.

Since 2012, I've been investing full-time in early-stage companies across India. I lead a syndicate on LetsVenture, advise IvyCap Ventures, and am an active member of the Indian Angel Network. But what I care about most is the hour I spend each week with a first-time founder who's figuring it out.

Transparency

13 write-offs and what they taught me.

I believe in being open about failures. Out of 75+ investments, 13 didn't make it. Each one taught me something I couldn't have learned from a win.

01
Market timing matters more than market size

Two companies I backed had genuinely large TAMs but arrived three years too early. The market wasn't ready, and the capital ran out before adoption caught up.

02
Co-founder conflict is the silent killer

Three of my write-offs trace back to founding team breakdowns. Now I spend as much time understanding the relationship between co-founders as I do the business model.

03
Unit economics don't lie — even when everything else does

In the growth-at-all-costs era, I learned the hard way that revenue without margins is just expensive storytelling. Now I look at contribution margins before GMV.
